The Stage 3 car level in Cadillacs and Dinosaurs ("The Wasteland") is one of the most memorable sequences in arcade history. It completely shifts the game from a slow-paced brawler into a fast, high-octane vehicular combat game using the exact same underlying 2D mechanics.How Capcom Implemented the Car LevelBecause Capcom's CPS-1 arcade hardware could not handle true 3D environments, they used visual tricks, forced scrolling, and modified collision boxes to create the illusion of high-speed highway combat.1. The Fake Speed Illusion (Forced Scrolling)The car isn't actually driving across a massive world map.Static Horizontal Position: The player’s car is locked to the left side of the screen. Pressing left or right on the joystick just shifts the car slightly forward or backward relative to the camera viewport.The Infinite Loop Background: The background layers (distant mountains, roadsides, and floor textures) scroll rapidly to the left on a fast, continuous loop.The Motorcycle Logic: Enemies on motorcycles spawn from the right side of the screen. Relative to the road, they are moving slower than the car, which makes them appear to "fall back" toward the car.2. Modified Collision and HitboxesIn a standard level, walking into an enemy does not hurt you; you must input a punch command. In Stage 3, Capcom flipped this logic:The Car is a Constant Hitbox: The front bumper of the car acts as a persistent, high-damage attack hitbox whenever the car has forward momentum.Motorcycle Vulnerability: The motorcycles have a specialized hurtbox. When the car's bumper hitbox overlaps with the motorcycle's hurtbox, the game registers a collision, instantly launching the enemy into a crash animation.Vertical (Y-Axis) Alignment: Because it is a beat-em-up, the road still has "depth" (the fake 3D Z-axis/Y-axis up and down). To hit a motorcycle, the player must align the car vertically on the same lane/track as the motorcycle.3. Boarding and Exiting MechanicsThe car has its own health bar.If the car takes too much damage from dynamite or boss attacks, it explodes, and the player sprite is thrown out onto the road.The background scroll speed instantly slows down to normal walking speed, and the game seamlessly transitions back into a standard foot-based beat-em-up.Best Practices for Designing Vehicular Combat in a Modern 2D GameTo recreate this smoothly in modern engines like Unity, Godot, or Unreal, follow this architectural setup:1. Use the "Static Object, Moving World" ArchitectureDo not move the player's car forward infinitely through world space, as this can cause floating-point precision errors over long distances. Instead, lock the world coordinates.Parallax Background Script: Create a script that scrolls your road and background textures along the X-axis based on a global CurrentSpeed variable.Clamp Car Movement: Restrict the player's Rigidbody or Transform component to a bounding box on the screen (e.g., between X: -8 and X: -4).2. Implement Lane/Depth Alignment (The 2.5D Solution)In modern 2D engines, you can handle the fake depth using actual 3D math or simulated 2D layers.Using 3D Space (Recommended): Set up your project in 3D, but lock the camera to an orthographic perspective. Give the car and motorcycles a 3D BoxCollider. This ensures that if a motorcycle is in the "top lane" and the car is in the "bottom lane," they pass each other safely because their Z-positions (depth) do not overlap.Using Pure 2D: Use Sorting Layers. Update the rendering order (SortingOrder) based on the character's vertical Y position (lower on the screen means closer to the camera, so it renders on top).3.
